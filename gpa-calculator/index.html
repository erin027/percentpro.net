Frequently asked questions

How is GPA calculated?

GPA is calculated by multiplying each course's grade points by its credit hours to get quality points, summing all quality points, then dividing by total credit hours.

Formula: GPA = Total Quality Points ÷ Total Credit Hours

Example: Math (4 credits, A = 4.0): 16 quality points. English (3 credits, B = 3.0): 9 quality points. Total = 25 ÷ 7 = 3.57 GPA.

What is a good GPA?

On a 4.0 scale: 3.5–4.0 is excellent (Dean's List territory), 3.0–3.4 is good, 2.5–2.9 is average, and below 2.0 may trigger academic probation. For graduate school, most programs want at least 3.0, with competitive programs expecting 3.5+.

What is the difference between semester GPA and cumulative GPA?

Semester GPA covers only courses from a single term — it resets each semester. Cumulative GPA is the weighted average of all courses taken across every semester. Your transcript shows both, but cumulative GPA is what employers and grad schools typically reference.

How do you raise your GPA?

Because GPA is weighted by credit hours, taking more high-credit courses and earning strong grades has the biggest impact. Retaking courses where you received a low grade (if your school allows grade replacement) is also effective. A single semester of all A's has less impact on a high credit-hour cumulative GPA than on a low one — the more credits you've completed, the harder it is to move the needle.

Does AP or IB coursework affect college GPA?

High school AP and IB grades do not affect your college GPA. College GPA starts fresh when you enroll. However, AP/IB exam scores may grant college credit, which can affect your total credit hours but typically not your GPA unless the credits are graded.
